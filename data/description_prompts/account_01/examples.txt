He managed $1.4 billion in client assets for six years.

"My bonus was tied to assets under management. Not returns," he told me.

He got paid more when clients deposited money. Not when investments grew.

The entire structure rewarded gathering, not performing. Every major firm works the same way.

The trick is called rebalancing.

Your advisor calls quarterly, says your portfolio drifted from target allocation. They move things around.

"Every rebalance generates a transaction. Every transaction generates a fee," he said.

Fees are buried in the expense ratio. Not on a separate line. Inside the fund itself.

The average client loses 1.2% annually to fees they never see on a statement.

I asked what 1.2% actually costs over time.

$500K invested for 25 years at 8% growth. Without fees: $3.4 million. With 1.2% drag: $2.2 million.

"Fees took a third of your retirement and you never noticed," he said.

Growth compounds for you. Fees compound against you.

Three index funds. Total expense ratio: 0.04%. No advisor. No quarterly calls.

"The finance industry is the only business where the customer pays more for a worse product and thanks the salesperson."

Active management underperforms indexing in 89% of 15-year windows. The data has been public since 2002.

He spent 22 years in financial compliance at JPMorgan Chase. His team monitored accounts for suspicious activity.

The threshold most people know is $10,000. But he said that number is a decoy.

Multiple deposits of $9,500. Round number transfers on repeating schedules. Sudden changes in account behavior.

"We caught more people avoiding the $10K limit than people who hit it," he told me.

His team flagged 340 accounts per month in one branch alone.

Your bank files one without telling you.

"No notification. No appeal. It sits in a federal database," he said.

Once filed, your account gets elevated monitoring for 18 months minimum. Every deposit, every wire gets a second look.

I asked what amount actually triggers a manual review.

"Anything that looks different from your last 90 days."

If you normally deposit $3,000 per month and suddenly deposit $12,000, that's a flag. Not the amount. The deviation from your pattern.

The algorithm compares your behavior to your own history.

Bank compliance protects institutions, not customers. The rules are public. The enforcement is private.
